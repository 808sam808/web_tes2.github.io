Iran's position at the conference differs from the statements made by other countries in several notable ways:

Sovereignty and Non-Interference: Iran emphasizes the sovereign right of each state to decide which international instruments to join and expresses skepticism about linking the implementation of the UN Programme of Action (UNPoA) with other non-consensual documents, such as the Arms Trade Treaty and the Firearms Protocol. This contrasts with some countries that advocate for a more integrated approach, suggesting that linking these frameworks could enhance overall effectiveness (Page 5).

Rejection of External Allegations: Iran categorically rejects allegations regarding its involvement in arms transfers that violate international law, asserting that it has not engaged in any activities contrary to its international obligations. This stance may differ from other countries that have raised concerns about Iran's role in regional conflicts and arms proliferation, reflecting a divergence in perspectives on accountability and compliance with international norms (Page 6).

Focus on Existing Frameworks: While many countries may support the introduction of new measures or frameworks to address the illicit arms trade, Iran advocates for a more cautious approach, urging a focus on refining and effectively implementing existing frameworks rather than expanding the scope with new concepts. This reflects a more conservative stance compared to those advocating for ambitious new initiatives (Page 4), (Page 5).

Concerns Over External Restrictions: Iran highlights the impact of unlawful restrictions imposed on it, which it claims hinder its ability to access necessary technical and technological assistance for combating illicit arms trade. This perspective may not be as prominently featured by other countries, which may focus more on collective challenges rather than specific national grievances (Page 2).

Pragmatic Recommendations: Iran calls for practical recommendations to address implementation challenges, emphasizing the need to focus on immediate, actionable solutions rather than theoretical discussions. This pragmatic approach may contrast with other delegations that might prioritize broader discussions on policy frameworks or theoretical underpinnings of arms control (Page 5), (Page 3).
